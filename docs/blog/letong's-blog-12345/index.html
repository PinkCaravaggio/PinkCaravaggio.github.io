The two spaces are connected by an archway woven from dried branches, evoking the principles of wabi-sabi—a reflection of Otani’s personal philosophy of living in harmony with nature. ⸻ Cultural Critique Visitors will first see the large English title Monsters in My Head once they enter the gallery, beside which the much smaller Japanese translation appears. The hierarchy between the two languages is not merely a visual design choice but a revelation of curatorial ideology. Monsters in My Head exemplifies the postcolonial dilemma of non-Western cultures that postcolonialism itself is a postmodern illusion grounded in Western ideology. This dilemma stems partly from the legacy of power structures of the Industrial Revolution and colonialism. Yet “the West” is not simply a geographical or political center—it has become deeply internalized as the foundational grammar of human thought, the epistemological structure underlying knowledge, law, art, philosophy, and education. It functions as the very medium of thought through which the subjectivity is constructed. The formal language, theoretical framework, and grammar of Monsters in My Head are all inscribed within a Western paradigm, replacing Japanese culture to the position of the object being spoken about. This dilemma is not only the result of collusion of power institutions, but also of the West’s absorptive capacity—its ability to appropriate through the rhetoric of inclusivity. Imagine how western discourse incorporates Otani with the gesture of being ‘neutral’ and ‘inclusive’. Since Romanticism, the Western avant-garde has built a tradition of rebellion so expansive that by the time of Duchamp’s Fountain, it had achieved an almost limitless capacity for inclusion. Such inclusivity, however, extends not only to avant-grade experimentation that rooted in tradition, but also to industrial mass culture and postcolonial global hybrid culture—
