云山图卷 THE SCROLL OF CLOUD & MOUNTAIN

This exhibition breaks the boundary between painting and installation through a contemporary fusion of both eastern and western visual theories and traditions, reintroducing the space as a field where the subject and viewers coexist with and reconstruct each other.

Artist Statement

云山图卷(the Scroll of Cloud & Mountain) is a contemporary extension of traditional Chinese landscape theory. The work experiments with new media and spatial form to expand the traditional paradigm of painting. By combining Chinese cosmology and painting theory with installation, space, and interaction, the project constructs a “field of qi” that departs from narrative and symbolic interpretation.

In this space, viewers are drawn into what I call a pure situation of encountering where narrative structures collapse; the work cannot simply be read or interpreted. It can only be entered, inhabited, sensed, and experienced relationally.

The installation consists of three interconnected elements: fragmented landscapes, rotating light structures, and fog.

The first element is a series of eight fragmented landscape paintings. Since the Song dynasty, landscape painting in China has functioned not merely as a subject matter but as a method of constructing cosmological models. By the Yuan dynasty, however, the collapse of social order led literati painters such as 倪瓒 (Ni Zan) to transform landscape into a projection of inner subjectivity. Yet even Ni Zan’s paintings preserved a coherent spatial structure: an inner cosmological model of an individual.

In my work, the landscape is no longer whole. Only scattered fragments remain. These traces reflect the fragmented and fluid condition of contemporary subjectivity. As viewers move through the installation, they continuously adjust their bodily position in relation to these fragments, composing their own shifting “landscape.” This mobile perception breaks the fixed perspective of traditional painting while ensuring that the work can never be seen in its entirety. It reveals how both I and others can only enter my state of existence partially and temporarily.

These landscape fragments are painted with ink on translucent fabric. Ink spreads rapidly across the surface and resists full control. Within the tension between restraint and loss of control, the bodily traces of the artist’s gesture—what Chinese aesthetics calls势 (shi, force or momentum)—condense into qi, the living condition of the present moment. The viewer is therefore no longer positioned as a distant observer but enters a responsive field of tensions.

As people walk through the space, they encounter one another, separate, intersect, and move again. Their bodies become moving images and flows of qi that continuously disturb and reshape the image. The boundary between viewer and image dissolves. In this sense, the work remains perpetually unfinished, reflecting the open-ended relational condition of the work.

This spatialization of landscape painting also reinterprets 宗炳 (Zong Bing)’s classical idea of “卧游(lying down to travel through the mountains).” Traditional painting sought to create a spiritual space in which viewers could mentally wander through the depicted world. In this installation, wandering becomes physical. The viewer’s body activates the movement of perception, turning space itself into a medium of painting.

The second element is a rotating light installation simulating the movement of the sun and moon. Designed and constructed in collaboration with my cousin Oliver. The counterclockwise rotation of orange and blue light rings visualizes the cyclical transformation of time through warm and cool illumination. Similar to Olafur Eliasson’s The Weather Project, this device brings cosmic order into the gallery space. Its fascination lies precisely in how it compresses the scale of the universe while enlarging the scale of the human senses, provoking a more intense, and perhaps entirely new experience of existence.

In traditional Chinese landscape painting, the sun and moon are not merely markers of time but portals of transformation of qi. Their presence alters the entire yin–yang structure of the universe. In this installation, this transformation affects not only the images but may also the viewers themselves. It suggests the presence of an order beyond fragmented individuals, which we constantly coexist and interact with.

The third element is fog. Fog fills the entire space, integrating all other components into a unified field (including viewers). In Classical Chinese landscape painting, clouds and mist function as negative space, generating qi through the interplay between presence and absence. Here, the blank space of painting expands outward into real space, the emptiness becomes fog. The translucent fabric appears almost immaterial, and the inky patterns dissolve into ambiguity, yet the dynamic balance between positive and negative space remains perceptible.

As a result, the work cannot be fully captured through images or documentation. It resists narrative consumption and instead invites viewers into a shifting field of perception, relation, and shared presence.

The fog emerges from beneath the wooden cabinet at the center of the space. As an inhabitable structure, the cabinet invites viewers to pause and dwell, offering a temporary point of stability within an ever-changing field of perception. It also becomes a kind of “stage” for viewers, that they may interact with the fog at their feet or sit alongside others. Since they are positioned at the center of the overall composition, they themselves become the subject of the work.

Rather than simply appropriating traditional imagery, the Scroll of Cloud & Mountain uses Chinese painting theory as a methodological foundation to rethink the boundaries of painting. By transforming landscape into a spatial and temporal situation, the work explores broader artistic questions shared across cultures: existence, time, space, embodiment, interaction, and the autonomy of viewers.
